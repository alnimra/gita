[Preface to the] Eighth Edition

 

 

Among the one hundred thousand slokas [verses] of the Mahabharata [epic poem of Hindu literature], seven hundred slokas make up the Srimadbhagavadgita [Bhagavad Gita], which, presented as a dialogue between Sri Krishna and Arjuna, is at once a doctrine of morality and duty, upanishad [philosophy on the nature of reality], Brahmavidya [spiritual treatise] and yogashastra [doctrine of spiritual practice].

 

The knowledge of Brahmavidya and yoga are learned from guru [preceptor]. In ancient times, only the fully renunciate yogis and sadhaks [spiritual practitioners] had the right and access [to these]. Householders did not. Because of the influence of dark times, the lineages of sadgurus [competent spiritual masters] were reduced to a very few, and those teachings nearly reached a state of extinction. Srimadbhagavadgita rescued that knowledge. It was not easy for householders to practice these teachings.

 

Because of the mercy of Babaji Maharaj, the Gurudev of Yogiraj Shyamacharan Lahiri Mahasaya, householders were availed the opportunity to learn – through the guru – brahmavidya: meaning vayu kriya (vayu Brahman)* [and] Rajayoga, termed concisely as Kriyayoga. One can practice this yoga after being initiated by a sadguru [spiritual teacher/preceptor]. Swami Pranabananda Giri, sannyasi [renunciate monk] disciple of Lahiri Mahasaya, had direct realization of the Spiritual Substance on which the Gita is based. He explained [the Gita's] spiritual teachings in a natural and simple language. Those teachings were published from Pranabashram in Varanasi by his disciple Sri Jnanendranath Mukhopadhyay in 1917 under the name of “Pranab Gita.” The experiences/perceptions [presented in this Gita] are [only attainable] through a formally learned and trained practice. No one should attempt to practice yoga on his/her own. Instruction by a knowledgeable person and sadguru is absolutely necessary. This dictum is written in many parts of the Pranab Gita.

 

[* Translators' note: The parentheses -”( )” - and the text within them are in the original.]

 

Swami Yogananda, one of the disciples of Swami Yukteshvar** Giri - another disciple of Lahiri Mahasaya – widely propagated this Kriyayoga in India and abroad. This Kriyayoga has been taught to many people in India, France, America [etc.]. His meeting with Swami Pranabananda and the Pranab Gita are written about in his book, “Autobiography of a Yogi.”

 

[** Translators' note: The name “Sriyukteshvar” is written as “Yukteshvar” in the original.]

 

Pranab Gita is hereby published again in whole. It is hoped that it will be received with interest by the spiritually inclined, [both] novice and experienced, as well as Kriyavan sadhaks [practitioners], researchers and scholars.

 

Sri Ramendranath Mukhopadhyay
